Introduction

This application has been designed to facilitate the interpretation, learning and use of soil classification according to the 12th edition of the Keys to Soil Taxonomy (pdf download). Through an agile and interactive access, it allows to identify the main properties of the soils up to the taxonomic level of the Great Group although it does not intend to replace the keys for the classification of the soils.

What is a soil?

Since 2017 there is a new definition of soil:
"The layer(s) of generally loose mineral and/or organic material that are affected by physical, chemical, and/or bio- logical processes at or near the planetary surface and usually hold liquids, gases, and biota and support plants" (van Es, 2017).
According to Soil Taxonomy the upper limit of soil is the boundary between soil and either air, shallow water, live plants, or plant materials that have not begun to decompose. Areas are not considered to have soil if the surface is permanently covered by water too deep (typically more than about 2.5 m) for the growth of rooted plants. The horizontal boundaries of soil are areas where the soil grades to deep water, barren areas, rock, or ice.
The lower boundary that separates soil from the nonsoil underneath is more difficult to define. Soil consists of the horizons near the surface that, in contrast to the underlying parent material, have been altered by the interactions of climate, relief, and living organisms over time. Commonly, soil grades at its lower boundary to hard rock or to earthy materials virtually devoid of animals, roots, or other marks of biological activity. The lowest depth of biological activity, however, is difficult to discern and is often gradual. For the practicality of soil survey, the lower boundary of soil is arbitrarily set at 200 cm.

Why are soils classified?

• To organize knowledge and allow the effective transmission of knowledge among different people.
• To facilitate research in a logical and understandable manner.
• To group them in similar classes according to some scientific or professional objective.
• To constitute, together with the Cartography, a base for practical applications.

Soil Taxonomy System

The Soil Taxonomy System has six levels of classification. They are, from most general to specific: order, suborder, great group, subgroup, family and series. The system is based on the definition of diagnostic horizons and characteristics for mineral and organic soils. Each horizon or diagnostic feature are defined one or several quantifiable properties whether they are: morphological (thickness, color, texture, etc.), physical (humidity and temperature regimes, among others), physicochemical (pH, base saturation, CEC, etc.), chemicals (carbon content, phosphates, etc.) or mineralogical (mineralogy of clays etc.).
The order is the highest level of hierarchy in the Soil Taxonomy classification system and is the level that uses the least amount of information for its definition. 12 orders are recognized. To classify a soil, the user of the system must begin by checking if the soil complies with all the properties required by the first Order of the key (Gelisols), in case of not fulfilling them, it must do the same with the following order (Histosols) and so on until the order is found for which the soil meets all the required properties.Once the soil is located within an order, the suborder is identified in the same way, then the great group, the subgroup and family until reaching the last taxonomic level that is the series, the taxa that uses the most amount of information for its definition. With this application you only get to the identification of the great group of a soil.

Sintax of a soil Great Group name.

The name at the level of Great Group is denominated with a single word constituted by three formative elements. Each formative element identifies the Order, Suborder and Great Group respectively. To identify each of the taxa that make up the name, the reading is done from right to left: the formative element located to the right of the word identifies the Order, the middle one, the Suborder and the left one the Great Group.

For example:

Argi ud oll


• oll : Order formative element: Mollisol
• ud : Suborder formative element: Udoll
• Argi : Great group formative element: Argiudoll
